What this dashboard shows

Every chart below is built from real daily pollution readings at Karnataka's official government monitoring stations (2023–2025). We compare each day's reading against India's legal air quality limit (NAAQS) to see how often a station breaches it, then look at what drives the pollution up or down — the time of year and the wind. Nothing here is predictive or diagnostic; it's a descriptive look at what the recorded data actually shows.

Winter vs monsoon PM2.5

Karnataka's pollution is strongly seasonal — low wind and winter inversions trap particulates; monsoon rain scrubs the air. Bars show each station's average PM2.5 in both seasons.
What this means: Two bars per station: how polluted the air typically is in winter (Nov–Feb) versus during monsoon season (Jun–Sep). In winter, calm air and cool temperatures trap pollution close to the ground. In monsoon, rain physically washes particles out of the air. Almost every station shows a much longer winter bar — meaning the same city's air can be dramatically worse in December than in July, even with no change in traffic or industry.

Wind speed effect on PM2.5

Estimated % change in PM2.5 per +1 m/s of wind speed, per station. Bars left of zero mean wind clears the air there; right of zero is a weak/inconsistent or counterintuitive relationship, often from low sample size or local geography.
What this means: Wind should blow pollution away — this chart checks whether that actually shows up in the data at each station. Green bars (pointing left) confirm the expected pattern: windier days had noticeably less PM2.5. Red bars (pointing right) either had little wind effect, or, counterintuitively, showed slightly more pollution on windy days — which can happen if wind is stirring up road dust locally, or if the station simply doesn't have enough measured days to draw a firm conclusion (hover a bar to see how many days it's based on).
